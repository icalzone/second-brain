# Credit Freeze
  
A credit freeze (or security freeze) restricts access to your credit report, making it nearly impossible for identity thieves to open new accounts in your name. It is a free, legally protected action that does not affect your credit score. When frozen, lenders cannot access your credit file to approve new loans or credit cards, acting as a "safe" for your credit.

**Steps:**  
    1. **Create accounts:** Set up an account with each bureau to manage the freeze.  
    1. **Provide details:** You will need your Social Security number, date of birth, and address history.  
    1. **Secure your PIN/Password:** You will create a PIN or password for each bureau to "thaw" (unfreeze) your credit later.  
    1. **Confirm:** Ensure you receive confirmation from all three bureaus.   
What a Credit Freeze Means  
    - **Protection:** It stops most new, unauthorized accounts from being opened.  
    - **No Credit Score Impact:** Freezing or unfreezing does not lower your credit score.  
    - **Temporary Thaw:** You can temporarily lift the freeze ("thaw") for a specific time when applying for legitimate credit, jobs, or renting an apartment.  
    - **Lasts Permanently:** The freeze stays in place until you remove it.  
    - **Not a Total Shield:** It does not prevent fraud on existing accounts (e.g., someone steals your credit card number).   
Credit Freeze vs. Credit Lock  
While both restrict access to your report, a **freeze** is legally mandated and free. A **lock** is a service provided by the bureaus that may carry a monthly fee and does not have the same legal protections as a freeze.   
*Note: You must freeze your credit with all three bureaus. Freezing only one is not sufficient, as lenders may pull from different databases.*
