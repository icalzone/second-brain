# National Park Timing — Best Months to Visit

Planning a national park visit requires balancing weather, crowd levels, and seasonal access. Each park has its own optimal window — some are best in spring, others peak in winter.

## General Seasonal Framework

| Season | Best For |
|---|---|
| **Spring (Mar–May)** | Wildflowers, waterfalls, fewer crowds, mild weather |
| **Summer (Jun–Aug)** | Full access, long days; most crowded and hottest |
| **Fall (Sep–Nov)** | Foliage, cooling temps, crowd drop-off |
| **Winter (Dec–Feb)** | Snow landscapes, solitude; limited access in many parks |

> The key principle: "A best month is one that isn't extremely busy, yet still offers great access, nice weather, and an overall enjoyable experience." — Bram Reusen

## Factors Used to Determine Best Times

1. **Historical visitor statistics** — peak visitation months (to avoid)
2. **Seasonal weather** — temperature ranges and precipitation
3. **Road/trail closures** — many mountain park roads close in winter
4. **Special events** — bat flights (Carlsbad Caverns), synchronous fireflies (Great Smoky Mountains), wildflower superbloom (Death Valley), bison calving (Yellowstone)

## Best Parks by Season

- **Spring:** Wildflower superbloom parks (Death Valley, Joshua Tree), waterfall parks (Yosemite, Smokies)
- **Summer island vacation parks:** Channel Islands, Dry Tortugas, Virgin Islands
- **Fall foliage:** Great Smoky Mountains, Shenandoah, Acadia
- **Winter wonderlands:** Yellowstone (snowmobiling), Bryce Canyon (snow among hoodoos), Grand Canyon South Rim
- **Warm winter getaways:** Everglades, Big Bend, Joshua Tree, Death Valley

## Reservations

Many popular parks now require advance reservations or timed entry permits during peak season:
- Yosemite, Zion, Arches, Rocky Mountain, Glacier (Going-to-the-Sun Road)
- Check [recreation.gov](https://www.recreation.gov) and [nps.gov](https://www.nps.gov) before any visit
