# Smartphone Photography

A practical guide to taking better photos with your iPhone or Android, covering camera setup, optimal settings, composition techniques, and recommended third-party apps. The core principle: master your phone's native camera first, then layer in apps.

## Understanding Your Camera Setup

Most smartphones have three cameras:

| Camera | Focal Length | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Main (1x)** | Wide | General use, macro close-ups |
| **Ultrawide (0.5x)** | Very wide | Landscapes; avoid for people (distorts faces) |
| **Telephoto (2x–5x)** | Zoom | Portraits, distant subjects |

- **Portrait mode** uses computational tricks; natural blur is better achieved by using the main or telephoto camera, zooming in, and stepping back.
- Stay at labeled zoom levels (0.5x, 1x, 2x, 5x) — in-between digital zooms (e.g., 2.7x) reduce quality.
- Above 10x zoom, photos often become blurry or pixelated.
- **Clean your lens** before any important shoot — grease ruins photos.

## Nailing Your Settings

### Focus & Exposure
- Tap to focus on the correct subject (important for group shots where AI may pick the wrong person).
- Tap a dark area → camera brightens; tap a bright area → camera darkens.
- **Drag the sun slider** (iPhone) or **brightness slider** (Pixel) to manually fine-tune exposure.
- **Lock focus & exposure:** Long-press on a subject for continuous shots of the same scene.

### Flash
- Avoid unless there is genuinely not enough light; flash looks unnatural.

### RAW Photos
- Available on most modern iPhones and Android devices.
- More natural look, no AI sharpening, but requires editing skill and takes significantly more storage.

### Quick Camera Access from Lock Screen
- **iPhone (modern):** Dedicated camera button on the right side — press once to open, again to shoot.
- **iPhone (older):** Swipe left from lock screen.
- **Pixel/Android:** Double-press power or volume button.

### Keep Settings Simple
> "No complex settings, no artificial portrait mode. I just occasionally adjust the brightness when necessary." — Nathanael Charpentier (pro photographer)

Focus on settings that matter: brightness, focus, and composition.

## Composition

### Rule of Thirds
Divide the frame into a 3x3 grid. Place key subjects near the **intersections** of those lines, not dead center. Enable the grid in Settings → Camera (iPhone: "Grid"; Pixel: "Grid Type → 3×3").

### When Symmetry Works
Centering is fine for converging lines (a dock, a road, a hallway) — then break the symmetry with a small element off-center.

### Other Techniques
- **Diagonal lines** — more dynamic than horizontal/vertical
- **Leading lines** — paths, roads, or rivers that guide the eye
- **Architectural perspective** — shoot upward at buildings for converging lines
- **Foreground framing** — use leaves, poles, or people to frame the distant subject
- **Low angle** — hold phone near ground level (or invert it) with ultrawide for drama
- **Shadows & contrast** — reduce exposure to make shadows dramatic; look for strong backlight for silhouettes
- **Macro** — get very close with the main or ultrawide camera; great for nature, food, and texture

### Exposure Tip from a Pro
> "Smartphones do almost everything well except scene exposure; usually the photos are too bright. That's why I very often correct the exposure of my photos." — Nathanael Charpentier

## Third-Party Apps

| App | Platform | Strengths |
|---|---|---|
| **VSCO** | iOS + Android | Exposure slider, separate focus/exposure, RAW support, filters; free + subscription |
| **Lightroom Mobile** | iOS + Android | Highlights overexposed areas, manual ISO/shutter speed/filters, post-edit suite; free + paid tiers |
| **Adobe Indigo** | iOS (experimental) | Computational photography for natural exposure/detail improvement |
| **Halide Mark II** | iOS only | Maximum manual control, RAW with zero AI, Depth Mode for any subject, $60 one-time or $20/yr |

> Practice the basics until you can confidently shoot in any situation, then layer in apps like Lightroom Mobile for the next level.
